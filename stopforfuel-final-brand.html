Amber — The "Look Here" Color

Brand marks, primary buttons, revenue numbers, active nav items, important totals. Use sparingly — 10-15% of screen area max. The less you use it, the more it pops.

Deep Navy — The Canvas

Dark mode backgrounds, sidebar, card surfaces. Covers 60-70% of dark UI. This is the stage — amber is the spotlight. Never pair deep navy text on dark backgrounds.

Teal — The Action Color

Secondary buttons, success states, toggles, chart accents, links. Pairs with amber without fighting it. Think of teal as "something good happened" or "tap here next."

Red — The Alarm

ONLY for: discrepancies, credit limit exceeded, overdue payments, delete actions. If red appears everywhere, nobody notices it when it matters. Guard this color jealously.

06 — Logo Usage Rules
Do's and Don'ts

Rules to keep the brand consistent everywhere it appears.

Do

  • Use the shield mark on dark backgrounds for maximum contrast
  • Keep clear space around the logo equal to the shield's width
  • Use the monochrome (white) version when printing on colored materials
  • Use the stacked wordmark when horizontal space is tight
  • Let the amber gradient flow top-to-bottom (light → dark)
  • Use "StopForFuel" as one word in the wordmark — no spaces

Don't

  • Rotate, skew, or distort the shield shape
  • Place the amber shield on a yellow or orange background
  • Add drop shadows, glows, or 3D effects to the logo
  • Change the gradient direction or colors
  • Stretch the logo non-proportionally
  • Use below 16px height — switch to simplified favicon mark instead
07 — Psychology
Why Employees Will Trust This

Every visual decision maps to a behavioral outcome.

🧠

Amber = Reward Signal

Gold/amber triggers the same "reward" pathways as actual gold. When a cashier sees their shift total in amber — it feels like achievement, not just a number. Payment apps (GPay, PhonePe) use this same trick.

🌙

Dark Mode = Focus

Dark backgrounds reduce visual fatigue during 8-hour shifts. Amber numbers on dark surfaces "glow" — the cockpit effect. Pilots, traders, and control rooms all use dark UIs for sustained attention.

✅

Green Check = Dopamine

When a shift closes cleanly, numbers match, payment clears — that green success indicator gives a micro-hit of satisfaction. Routine work becomes "completing a task" that feels genuinely good.

🛡️

Shield = "This is Mine"

The shield subconsciously communicates ownership and protection. Employees who feel ownership over their tools treat them with care. They don't just use the app — they depend on it.

🔁

Consistency = Habit

Same colors, same fonts, same icon — on web, on phone, on every screen. The brain stops "learning the interface" and starts "just using it." That's when software becomes invisible — the highest compliment.

📐

Clean Layout = Competence

When the UI looks organized, employees assume the data is accurate. Messy design → "can I trust these numbers?" Clean design → "yeah, that looks right." Perception drives trust.
